This means that up until January, if you were a user of their OnStar Smart Driving feature, your geolocation and driving behavior data were likely being sold to consumer reporting agencies without your knowledge. It means that the company that owns your car is currently lobbying against a bill in congress that would ensure your "right to repair" by mandating that any data generated by your vehicle's software be accessible to you and anybody of your choosing at any time. If you live in Detroit and gaze upon the Renaissance Center standing against the river, GM owns your skyline too, and is currently threatening to demolish your city if they do not receive a tax grant to renovate their properties. They control your present and shape your future, even if you do not own any of their products; this is their modern monopoly, a monopoly on your life.

General Motors' History

The more you learn about General Motors' history, the more you know about American history. The company's history is long, and it includes some rather influential individuals as well. The following is a list of sources that paint a comprehensive picture of it as you compare what is recounted, and what isn't, in each telling of General Motors' story.

  • General Motors' Wikipedia Page

    Wikipedia is an excellent beginning to a research journey. GM's profile is listed in great detail here, and you can use listed references to find more concrete biographies or look deeper into topics that catch your attention.

  • General Motors' Website

    There is a lot that can be gleaned from what a person says about themselves when asked to talk about their background. Corporations are the same; for a company with such a long history, what does General Motors include and what do they omit when retelling it themselves?

  • Nomination of Durant-Dort Carriage Company Office for the NRHPI

    The founder of General Motors, William Crapo Durand, was a very interesting person with a very interesting life. Looking at the people and practices that began such a cataclysmic corporation can shed light onto the morals and methodology the company we know today. I found this form nominating Durand's original Durant-Dort Carriage Company Office for the National Register of Historic Places Inventory a particularly intriguing and in-depth biographical source, among many others.

  • Alfred P. Sloan Jr. of the Automotive Hall of Fame

    Sloan was William Durand's successor, and the individual responsible for the General Motor's Streetcar Conspiracy. His profile in the Automotive Hall of Fame does not mention this, but it does provide a timeline of Sloan's career and illustrates what of his accomplishments GM is eager to wear on their sleeve.

It Could Have Been Great

General Motors would not be where it is today without their conspiracy to end public transportation. Before the second World War only one in ten Americans owned a car and the rest didn't need one, but GM created a market for their product by buying and replacing electric streetcars with motorized buses, then smothering the budget of this system until it could no longer be used, at which point they offered a glamorous solution: personal vehicles to take the modern, independent American anywhere you may need to go. The documentary "Taken For a Ride" tells this story in first-hand detail, and now can only be found on YouTube. I recommend watching it to find out why grass grows over train tracks in your town and the bus is never on time; this is history you deserve to know before it is slowly etched off of the face of the internet.
